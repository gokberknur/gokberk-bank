# Lending — competitive benchmarks

How the best present credit, so gökberk bank can match or beat them. Use this to calibrate "how good does this
have to be?" — the answer is usually "clearer than the incumbent mortgage lender, as honest as the best
neobank, with none of the BNPL nudging."

## The bar-setters

- **Trade Republic** — editorial calm applied to money. Restrained, numerals-first, no hype; credit and
  interest presented as plain fact. Closest in spirit to gökberk's voice. Match the calm; add the full
  regulated disclosure.
- **Klarna** — the master of *frictionless* credit, for better and worse. The strength: an instant, legible
  "here's what you'll pay" at the point of decision, small clear instalments. The trap to avoid: minimising the
  cost of credit and nudging the customer past affordability. We borrow the legibility, not the nudge — and we
  show APR + the representative example where Klarna shows only the instalment.
- **Modern digital mortgage lenders (Habito, Trussle, Better-style)** — turned the dreaded mortgage process
  into a guided, transparent flow: live calculator, clear LTV bands, a believable monthly, documents uploaded
  not posted. Match the guidance and the calculator; keep the ESIS-grade disclosure.
- **Neobanks doing loans (Revolut, N26, Monzo)** — a pre-approved amount, a clean amount/term slider, a one-
  screen offer, instant-ish funding. Fast and clear. Match the slider-to-offer speed; never drop the total cost
  of credit to get there.
- **Traditional banks (incumbents)** — the contrast: a rate with no APR next to it, fees discovered at signing,
  a decline that's a flat "no", a mortgage statement that's a wall of numbers with no schedule view, an early-
  repayment charge sprung as a surprise. We win by disclosing everything up front and declining like humans.

## Patterns worth stealing

- **The live calculator before the application** (digital mortgage lenders) — drag the inputs, watch the
  monthly + total interest update, *then* decide to apply. Shareable by URL.
- **The one-screen offer ledger** (neobanks) — APR/APRC, monthly, term, total repayable, total cost of credit,
  first-payment date, all on one calm card, with the representative example beside it.
- **Klarna's "what you'll pay" legibility** — the exact number at the moment of decision, in plain figures.
- **An amortization view that shows the front-loaded interest** (good mortgage tools) — a chart of balance-to-
  zero and the principal/interest split, so the cost over time is *seen*.
- **Overpayment effect preview** (better servicing tools) — "this shortens your term by 4 months" before
  confirm; the saving made visible.
- **Decline-with-alternatives** (the better lenders) — a smaller amount, a longer term, a guarantor, check
  again later — instead of a flat refusal.

## Anti-patterns to avoid (where incumbents and BNPL both fail)

- A headline rate with **no APR/APRC** beside it.
- Fees **discovered at signing**, not itemised on the offer.
- "**0% / best rate!!**" teaser hype with the real cost in fine print.
- BNPL-style **nudging past affordability** — minimising the commitment to win the "yes".
- A **decline as a verdict** — a flat "no", or worse, a credit score shown as a judgement.
- A mortgage statement with **no schedule view**, or a 360-row table that janks the page.
- An **early-repayment charge sprung** at confirmation instead of disclosed before the overpayment.
- Marking a payoff **"closed" before it clears** — faking finality.

## The gökberk angle

Match the neobanks on speed and the digital mortgage lenders on guidance; **beat** everyone on honesty and
calm. Where Klarna shows the instalment, we show the instalment *and* the APR *and* the representative example —
and we never nudge. Where the incumbent springs a fee, we itemise it on the offer. Where a traditional lender
declines with a flat "no", we give a plain reason and a real door. The differentiator isn't a cheaper rate;
it's a loan you understand *completely* — and a decline you don't take personally — before anything is signed.
